Adaptive Dual MA Trend Ribbon Community “Larsson-Style” Presets

A dual-moving-average “trend ribbon” for TradingView that allows traders to experiment with SMMA / EMA / SMA / WMA pairs and optional ATR-based neutral zones. The indicator is free to use on TradingView; the source code itself is published as protected.

How Traders Recreate This Without the Indicator

At its core, many so-called “Larsson-style” trend ribbons are based on a very simple idea: plotting two smoothed moving averages and observing how they interact over time.

If you want to recreate a similar structure manually in TradingView—without using this indicator—you can do the following:

  1. Add a Smoothed Moving Average (SMMA) to your chart.
  2. Set its length to 15 and leave the remaining inputs at their defaults.
  3. Add a second Smoothed Moving Average with the length set to 29.
  4. When the faster SMMA (15) moves above the slower SMMA (29), the trend bias is bullish.
  5. When the faster SMMA (15) moves below the slower SMMA (29), the trend bias is bearish.

Because moving averages are lagging by nature, this approach tends to work best on higher timeframes (daily and above) and during clear directional trends. It performs poorly in sideways or choppy markets, which is why many traders combine it with volatility filters, market structure, or other forms of confirmation.

The indicator provided here simply packages this concept into a cleaner visual ribbon, adds optional ATR-based neutral zones, and makes it easier to experiment with different moving-average types and lengths. You are free to explore and adjust these parameters as much as you like.

Overview

Many traders enjoy experimenting with dual-moving-average “trend ribbon” structures that resemble the visual style of popular tools such as the Larsson Line. While the internal logic of closed-source indicators is not publicly available, the trading community has long explored MA combinations like SMMA 15/29 or EMA 30/60 with ATR filters to study trend transitions and visualize momentum shifts.

This indicator does not replicate, reverse-engineer, or replace any closed-source tool. Instead, it provides a flexible framework that allows traders to build their own trend-ribbon configuration inspired by similar visual concepts, while remaining transparent about inputs and behavior.

What the Script Does

  • Plots Fast and Slow moving averages using SMMA (RMA), EMA, SMA, or WMA.
  • Colors the ribbon to show directional bias:
    • Bullish when Fast MA > Slow MA
    • Bearish when Fast MA < Slow MA
    • Neutral when the difference is small (optional ATR filter)
  • Supports ATR-based neutral zone filtering to highlight choppy conditions.
  • Supports optional bar coloring by trend state.
  • Works on any market and timeframe.
  • Free public indicator; code is protected on TradingView.

Why This Script Is Useful

Traditional moving averages identify trend direction but often lack context during uncertain or low-momentum conditions. This ribbon adds structure in several ways:

  1. Allows multiple smoothing techniques (SMMA / EMA / SMA / WMA).
  2. Highlights clearer transitions between bullish, bearish, and neutral phases.
  3. Uses ATR to flag low-confidence, sideways periods.
  4. Provides a visually intuitive band instead of a single line.

It is well-suited for swing traders, trend followers, and breakout traders who want a clean context filter to complement—not replace—their existing entry and risk rules.

Common Community Presets

  • SMMA 15 / 29 – smoother structural trend flow.
  • EMA 30 / 60 – more responsive momentum shifts.
  • EMA 10 / 21 – intraday rhythm.
  • EMA 50 / 100 – higher-timeframe structure.

These settings are commonly discussed within the trading community, but the indicator does not attempt to recreate or imitate any closed-source or commercial product.

How to Use

  1. Choose your preferred moving-average type and lengths based on timeframe.
  2. Enable the ATR Neutral Zone if you want fewer noisy flips.
  3. Optionally color bars by trend for faster visual interpretation.
  4. Combine with structure, volume, and your own entry logic for confluence.
